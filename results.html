Question 1: How often do you eat fast food in a typical week?

According to this website, roughly one Big Mac meal results in 2.35kg of carbon emissions being emitted into the atmosphere. The website is dedicated to a vegetarian lifestyle, ensuring the accuracy of these references.

Additionally, a research paper indicates the carbon emissions of eating food at home versus eating at a restaurant or takeaway. Considering that food must be consumed regardless, the emissions from food eaten at home were subtracted from those at a takeaway, resulting in an average emissions rate of around 1.6kg of CO2 per kg of food.

Based on these sources, the average between both values is assumed to represent the total carbon emissions for each meal outside of your home, which amounts to approximately 1.975kg or 2kg of CO2.

Therefore, if you were to order takeaway, once a week, this would equate to around 104 Kilograms of C02 being emitted into the atmosphere.To put that into perspective, this is the same as if you were to drive 937 kilometres in a car!

Question 2: On average, how many hours per day do you spend using electronic devices?

According to this website, it says that by using your phone for only one hour a day leads to 63 kilograms a year of carbon emissions being produced into the atmosphere.

63 kilograms is about the same as driving 568 kilometres in a car. Its very scary to think about that by using your phone for an hour each day over a year, is equal to driving that much distance...

Question 3: How often do you charge your electronic devices?

According to this website, in Europe, if you charge your phone once a day the average estimate of carbon emissions is around 0.54kg per year. That does not really seem like a lot. I will agree, that as an individual, it does not. But this source also shows how much carbon is produced if EVERY phone was charging at the same time, even if only for an hour. This would lead to roughly 8,088,324 TONNES. And that is only once, for an hour. What about if you left your phone charging overnight....

Question 4: On average, how many kilometres would you drive/be driven in a week?

The SEAI gives us a rough estimate of how much carbon emissions are produce per kilometre travelled. 111g per kilometre. As students, perhaps you might not be driving as much as adults, but it is important to consider for those who drive to school rather than taking the bus, cycling or walking.

Let us assume that you drive or are driven around 50 km each week. That is not a lot in comparison to other people who drive, but for arguments sake, let us make that assumption. That equals around 2600 kilometres a year. That is very little for someone who might be driving every day. But let us put that into more perspective.

2600 km is around 288 kg of C02 per year, which in turn is about the same as charging your phone 528 times!

Question 5: How often do you turn off the lights when you are not in the room?

This study sheds light on the carbon emissions resulting from leaving lights on, drawing from research in the UK. It suggests that an average of approximately 5.75 kg of carbon emissions is generated per year if lights are constantly left on. As we have a similar power grid to that of the UK, this can be estimated to be accurate to a certain degree for Ireland. When applied to millions of households across the country, this seemingly small amount of emissions accumulates significantly.

Question 6: How often do you take a shower/bath?

BBVA states that an average shower that lasts around 5 minutes has a maximum carbon emission of around 200 grams. If one was to shower every day, thats around 73 kg per year. According to a study on trees, the average amount a tree negates C02 in a year is around 21-31kg of C02. For these stats, this would only be 2-3 trees each year for all the showers one would have in a year.

However, these results for how much carbon is produced for a shower only takes into account for a 5 minute shower with a gas boiler. Electric boilers cost more and there is always a possibility that one showers for longer than 5 minutes.

Question 8: How often do you but new jeans or trousers?

Question 9: Do you have a reusable water bottle?

This document shows you how much one 500ml bottle emits in carbon emissions and it boils down to roughly 83 grams per bottle. Over time, this adds up a lot if you were to drink 2 litres a day. Therefore the most viable option is a reusable water bottle. While they do create more carbon emissions to create, they break even around 40-50 refills.
