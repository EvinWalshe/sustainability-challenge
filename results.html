Question 1: How often do you eat fast food in a typical week?

Fast Food vs. Food Cooked at Home

According to this study, the average consumption of Food Away From Home (FAFH), or fast food, is roughly 4.3 kg of CO2 per kg of food. Food cooked at home (FAH) is around 2.7 kg of CO2 per kg of food. This results in a difference of 1.6 kg of CO2 per kg of food between fast food and home-cooked food.

Carbon Emissions of Specific Fast Food Items

Carbon emissions for one Big Mac are estimated to be 2.35 kg. The average weight of a Big Mac is 240 grams according to McDonald's. Therefore, the average carbon emissions per gram of a Big Mac can be calculated as (1.6+ 2.35) = 1.975 kg / 240 grams = 8.22 grams of CO2 per gram of food.

Carbon Emissions from Eating Out

Mcdonalds also claim that all their food in Ireland, comes from Irish sources so all the above calculations could in theory be applied to other fast food restaurants. Based on the above calculations, we will find the average weight of a general fast food takeway: Fish and chips at 450 grams. A medium big mac burger and chips comes to 354 grams (240 + 114). Therefore, we can find the average size of a takeway to be (450 + 354)/2 = 402 grams. Multiplied by the average carbon emission per gram will then give us an estimate of how much carbon is released for a takeaway. 8.22 * 402 = 3.304 kg / CO2 per takeaway.

Conclusion

In summary, opting for home-cooked meals over fast food can significantly reduce carbon emissions. While fast food may be convenient, it carries a substantial environmental cost. Choosing sustainable options and advocating for eco-friendly practices within the fast food industry can help mitigate its impact on the environment.

Question 6: How often do you take a shower/bath?

How much a shower produces

Flow rates of a shower can have different flow rates depending on whether the system is high pressure or not. For this example however, a flow rate of 15 Litres/min will be chosen. At 5 minutes a shower this ends up at 75 Liters of water per shower.

Calculations

A modern A-rated gas boiler will emit 215 grams of CO2 / kWh of heat. Next, we need to convert the volume of water (75 liters) into energy used to heat it. Assuming the water starts at around 10°C (typical cold water temperature), and we want it to reach a comfortable shower temperature of around 38°C, we'll need to heat it by:
38°C - 10°C = 28°C
Energy = mass of water * specific heat capacity * temperature change
75 litres * 1000 grams/litre * 4.186 J/g°C * 28°C = 8,790,600 Joules
Now, we need to convert this energy from Joules to kWh (kiloWatt-hours), since the CO2 emissions are typically measured per kWh.
1 kWh = 3.6 × 10^6 Joules
8,790,600 Joules / (3.6 × 10^6 Joules/kWh) = 2.4425 kWh
CO2 emissions per kWh = 215 grams
CO2 emissions for the shower = Energy used * CO2 emissions per kWh
CO2 emissions for the shower = 2.4425 kWh * 215 grams/kWh = 525.1375 grams

Question 9: Do you have a reusable water bottle?

Re-usable vs Plastic

CarbonCloud is a company that has spent the past 20 years dedicating itself to creating software that calculates the carbon footprint of items. They claim, the 1 Litre PET water bottle (plastic) creates 280 grams per bottle.
In 2012, Beverage Industry Environmental Roundtable claimes that it only costs 163 grams per 1.5 Litre bottle.
How Bad Are Bananas? states that a plastic bottle is 103 grams.
So which one is the most accurate? Well that is hard to prove fully, so for this argument, we will assume it is only 103 grams per 1 litre plastic bottle.

Nestle carried out a study in 2010 to try and find out how much a reusable bottle beats a plastic bottle? They found that, depending on the material the reusable bottle is made from, creating a reusable bottle produces just under 2kg of CO2. So, by that logic, for a reusable bottle to break even on the "most" sustainable plastic bottle of 103 grams, the reusable bottle would need to be filled 20 times.
This does not include the life-cycle of the reusable bottle but solely the buying and use of the bottles.
